Timeless Jeweler App

Ruby on Rails, PostgreSQL, StimulusReflex, Bootstrap 5, Heroku
Project Overview
I developed a custom retail management app for a client in the jewelry industry that stores, analyzes, and displays information related to inventory, customers, and services.
My Contributions
Whereas most jewelry retail management solutions use a generic inventory model which fails to account for the idiosyncrasies of the domain, I designed and implemented a data model that fits these idiosyncrasies much more closely, resulting in a far more accurate database.
Client Background
Small retail jeweler
Timeless Jeweler is a small, growing retail jewelery store in Salem, OR. Operated by a savvy sole proprietor and fueled by acute business sense, strong networking, a thriving Facebook page, and an in-store mascot in the form of a chihuahua, the store had grown steadily over the past few years and was starting to feel growth pains as it maximized its current operations.

The store had previously managed its retail operations by hand, tracking inventory and sales on paper. The owner, Doug, routinely won customer loyalty by recalling ring sizes, anniversaries, birthdays, and color preferences without having to ask, but he knew he would not be able to continue to do so on his own as his customer base continued to grow. He approached me for help migrating his operations onto a digital platform.
Technical Challenges
Confronting the O/R impedance mismatch
Whereas most jewelry retail management solutions use a generic inventory model which fails to account for the idiosyncrasies of the domain, I designed and implemented a data model that fits these idiosyncrasies much more closely, using a combination of domain and formal analysis. This resulted in a far more accurate database.

Normalizing the data model resulted in good database elements such as lookup tables, check constraints, and join tables, but it did provide additional challenges at the Rails application layer. Here I butted heads with the Object-Relational Impedance mismatch and won only by a narrow margin.

For the user interface, I adapted a premium Bootstrap theme called Dashkit. I also utilized the new View Component gem from GitHub, which allows for proper testing of view components, better encapsulation of data in the view layer, and easier re-usability across the project.

To allow the user to interact with the complex data model, I had to employ forms with deeply nested attributes and render fields conditionally using JavaScript.
Testing the application
Test suite overview
The application test suite uses Cucumber for high-level acceptance tests and Minitest with Thoughtbot's Shoulda Matchers for low-level unit tests.

Shoulda Matchers were invaluable for testing the numerous associations and validations of models, especially those with custom class names. They allowed me to experiment confidently with namespacing and other high-level refactorings until I arrived at a model directory structure that worked best.
